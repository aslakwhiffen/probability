
With $ \mu = 0$, the moment generating function $ M_{X} (t) $ is

\[
    M_{X} (t) = e^{ \frac{\sigma^2}{2} t} 
.\] 

We can expand the exponential into a power series

\begin{align*}
    e^{ \frac{\sigma^2}{2} t} &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \left( \sigma^2 \frac{t^2}{2}
    \right)^{n} \\
			      &= 1 + 0 + \frac{1}{2!} \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{2}  \right)^2 t^{2}  +
			      0 \dots
\end{align*}

Based on our assumption 

\[
    M_{X} (t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \E(X^{n} ) t^{n}  
\] 
and the fact that two power series represent the same function only if they
have the same coefficients, we get that for even $ n$ (considering the term with $ t^{n} $ in both
power series)

\begin{align*}
    \frac{1}{n!} t^{n}  \E X^{n} &= \frac{1}{(n/2)!} t^{n} \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{2}  \right)^{n/2}  
\end{align*}
and for odd $ n$

\begin{align*}
    \frac{1}{n!} t^{n} \E X^{n} &= 0. 
\end{align*}
This means that 

\begin{align*}
    \E X^{n} &= \begin{cases}
	0 & , n \, \mbox{is odd} \\
	\sigma^{2k} \frac{(2k)!}{2^{k} k!} &, n = 2k \, \mbox{is even} 
    \end{cases}
\end{align*}
